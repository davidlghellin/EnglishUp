# American vs British Pronunciation

## Focusing on American English

---

## Key Differences

### The "R" Sound

**American English**: The "R" is always pronounced (rhotic)
**British English**: The "R" is often silent after vowels (non-rhotic)

| Word | American 🇺🇸 | British 🇬🇧 |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| car | /kɑːr/ (R pronounced) | /kɑː/ (R silent) |
| water | /ˈwɑːtər/ | /ˈwɔːtə/ |
| teacher | /ˈtiːtʃər/ | /ˈtiːtʃə/ |
| here | /hɪr/ | /hɪə/ |
| better | /ˈbetər/ | /ˈbetə/ |
| four | /fɔːr/ | /fɔː/ |

**Tip**: In American English, always pronounce the R at the end of words!

---

### The "T" Sound (Flapping)

In American English, "T" between vowels sounds like a soft "D":

| Word | American 🇺🇸 | British 🇬🇧 |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| water | "wader" /ˈwɑːdər/ | "water" /ˈwɔːtə/ |
| better | "bedder" /ˈbedər/ | "better" /ˈbetə/ |
| butter | "budder" /ˈbʌdər/ | "butter" /ˈbʌtə/ |
| letter | "ledder" /ˈledər/ | "letter" /ˈletə/ |
| city | "cidy" /ˈsɪdi/ | "city" /ˈsɪti/ |
| party | "pardy" /ˈpɑːrdi/ | "party" /ˈpɑːti/ |
| meeting | "meeding" /ˈmiːdɪŋ/ | "meeting" /ˈmiːtɪŋ/ |

**Practice:**
- I need some wa**t**er → wa**d**er
- The wea**t**her is be**tt**er → wea**d**er, be**d**er
- Le**t** me ge**t** i**t** → le**d** me ge**d** i**d**

### The "A" Sound

| Word | American 🇺🇸 | British 🇬🇧 |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| can't | /kænt/ (sounds like "ant") | /kɑːnt/ (sounds like "arnt") |
| dance | /dæns/ | /dɑːns/ |
| ask | /æsk/ | /ɑːsk/ |
| class | /klæs/ | /klɑːs/ |
| path | /pæθ/ | /pɑːθ/ |
| bath | /bæθ/ | /bɑːθ/ |
| laugh | /læf/ | /lɑːf/ |
| half | /hæf/ | /hɑːf/ |

**American "a"** = flat, like in "cat"
**British "a"** = long, like "ah"

### Schedule

This word is very different!

| | Pronunciation |
|---|---------------|
| American 🇺🇸 | **/ˈskedʒ.uːl/** - "SKED-jool" |
| British 🇬🇧 | /ˈʃedjuːl/ - "SHED-yool" |

**Practice the American version:**
- What's your **schedule** today? → "SKED-jool"
- Let me check my **schedule** → "SKED-jool"

## American Intonation

### Rising and Falling Patterns

**American English tends to:**
- Have more variation in pitch
- Use uptalk (rising at the end) in questions
- Have a more "musical" rhythm

**Example - Statement vs Question:**
- "You're coming." ↘ (falling = statement)
- "You're coming?" ↗ (rising = question)

### Stress in Sentences

Americans often stress:
- Content words (nouns, verbs, adjectives)
- New or important information

**Example:**
- "I **NEED** to **TALK** to you about the **MEETING**."
- (need, talk, meeting = stressed)

## American Contractions and Reductions

Americans frequently reduce words in casual speech:

| Written | American casual |
|---------|-----------------|
| going to | **gonna** /ˈɡʌnə/ |
| want to | **wanna** /ˈwɑnə/ |
| have to | **hafta** /ˈhæftə/ |
| got to | **gotta** /ˈɡɑːtə/ |
| kind of | **kinda** /ˈkaɪndə/ |
| sort of | **sorta** /ˈsɔːrtə/ |
| don't know | **dunno** /dəˈnoʊ/ |
| let me | **lemme** /ˈlemi/ |
| give me | **gimme** /ˈɡɪmi/ |

**Examples:**
- I'm **gonna** go to the store. (= going to)
- I **wanna** talk to you. (= want to)
- You've **gotta** see this! (= got to)
- It's **kinda** hard to explain. (= kind of)
- I **dunno** what to do. (= don't know)

> **Note**: Use these in casual speech, not formal writing!

## Words Starting with S (American Style)

Remember: NO extra vowel before S + consonant!

| Wrong ❌ | Correct ✅ | American pronunciation |
|----------|-----------|------------------------|
| e-strategy | **strategy** | /ˈstrætədʒi/ |
| e-school | **school** | /skuːl/ |
| e-speak | **speak** | /spiːk/ |
| e-stage | **stage** | /steɪdʒ/ |
| e-schedule | **schedule** | /ˈskedʒuːl/ |
| e-start | **start** | /stɑːrt/ |
| e-snake | **snake** | /sneɪk/ |
| e-Spain | **Spain** | /speɪn/ |

**Practice sentences (American style):**
1. The **strategy** meeting is on **schedule**.
2. I **spoke** at a **stage** in front of many people.
3. **School** **starts** at 8am.
4. Can you **speak** **slower**, please?

## Practice Exercises

### Exercise 1: Pronounce these words American-style

1. water → /ˈwɑːdər/ (wa-der)
2. better → /ˈbedər/ (bed-er)
3. schedule → /ˈskedʒuːl/ (SKED-jool)
4. data → /ˈdeɪtə/ (DAY-ta)
5. can't → /kænt/ (like "ant")

### Exercise 2: American reductions

Convert to casual American:

1. I am going to call you → I'm **gonna** call you
2. I want to go home → I **wanna** go home
3. I have to work late → I **hafta** work late
4. It is kind of cold → It's **kinda** cold
5. I do not know → I **dunno**

### Exercise 3: Practice the flapped T

Say these pairs - the T should sound like a soft D:

1. writer / rider (sound almost the same!)
2. latter / ladder (sound almost the same!)
3. waiting → waiding
4. meeting → meeding
5. sitting → sidding

## Tips for American Accent

1. **Always pronounce the R** - car, here, water, better
2. **Flap the T** between vowels - water sounds like "wader"
3. **Use the flat "a"** - can't, dance, ask (like "cat")
4. **Reduce common phrases** - gonna, wanna, gotta
5. **Keep a steady rhythm** - don't stop to translate
6. **Listen to American media** - podcasts, Netflix, YouTube

### Recommended Resources

- **Podcasts**: NPR, This American Life
- **TV Shows**: Friends, The Office (US), Modern Family
- **YouTube**: Rachel's English, Accent's Way

---

**Remember**: The goal is to be understood, not to have a perfect accent. Focus on the key differences that affect comprehension: R sounds, T flapping, and S + consonant words.
